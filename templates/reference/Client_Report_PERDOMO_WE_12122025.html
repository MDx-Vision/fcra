Section 2: Understanding Your Rights

Section 2: Understanding Your Rights

Before we dive into the specific violations found in your credit reports, it's important to understand your rights under federal law. The Fair Credit Reporting Act (FCRA) is a powerful consumer protection law that gives you significant rights ‚Äî and provides for substantial penalties when those rights are violated.

What is the Fair Credit Reporting Act?

The Fair Credit Reporting Act (15 U.S.C. ¬ß 1681 et seq.) was enacted by Congress in 1970 and has been strengthened several times since. It regulates how consumer credit information is collected, maintained, and shared.

The FCRA recognizes that your credit report is one of the most important documents affecting your financial life. It determines:

  • Whether you can get a mortgage, car loan, or credit card
  • What interest rates you'll pay
  • Whether you can rent an apartment
  • Whether you can get certain jobs
  • What you'll pay for insurance

Because inaccurate credit information can cause such significant harm, Congress created strict requirements for accuracy and gave consumers powerful tools to fight back when errors occur.

Your Core Rights Under the FCRA

1. The Right to Accurate Information (¬ß 1681e(b))

Credit bureaus must use "reasonable procedures to assure maximum possible accuracy" of your credit information. This means they can't just blindly publish whatever data furnishers send them ‚Äî they must have quality control procedures in place.

2. The Right to Dispute Inaccurate Information (¬ß 1681i)

You have the right to dispute any information you believe is inaccurate. When you do, credit bureaus must investigate within 30 days and correct or delete information that cannot be verified.

3. The Right to Know What's in Your File (¬ß 1681g)

You're entitled to one free credit report per year from each bureau, plus free reports whenever you're denied credit or when certain other conditions are met.

4. The Right to Sue for Violations (¬ß 1681n, ¬ß 1681o)

If credit bureaus or data furnishers violate your rights, you can sue them in federal court and recover:

  • Statutory damages: $100 to $1,000 per violation (for willful violations)
  • Punitive damages: Additional amounts to punish bad behavior
  • Actual damages: Compensation for real harm you suffered
  • Attorney fees: The defendant must pay your lawyer
Why This Matters for Your Case

The violations we found in your credit reports aren't just minor errors ‚Äî they represent failures to follow the law. When we found 9 accounts reported as both "Open" and "Paid" simultaneously, that's not a reasonable mistake. It's evidence that someone isn't following their legal obligations.

Who Must Follow the FCRA?

Credit Reporting Agencies (CRAs)

Companies like Equifax, Experian, and TransUnion that compile and sell credit information. They must:

  • Use reasonable procedures to ensure accuracy
  • Investigate disputes within 30 days
  • Delete information that can't be verified
  • Not report obsolete information (generally 7 years old)

Data Furnishers

Companies like banks, credit card issuers, and lenders that report your payment history to credit bureaus. They must:

  • Report accurate information
  • Investigate disputes forwarded by credit bureaus
  • Correct or delete inaccurate information they reported
  • Not report information they know is inaccurate

What Makes a Violation "Willful"?

Under the Supreme Court's decision in Safeco Insurance Co. v. Burr, a violation is "willful" if:

  1. The defendant knew they were violating the law, OR
  2. The defendant acted with "reckless disregard" for the law ‚Äî meaning their interpretation of the law was objectively unreasonable

They're objectively impossible scenarios that sophisticated companies like Chase, Equifax, and Nationstar should have caught. This pattern suggests reckless disregard for accuracy, which supports willful violation claims.

The Metro 2¬Æ Format: Industry Standards

All major data furnishers report credit information using a standardized format called Metro 2¬Æ, maintained by the Consumer Data Industry Association (CDIA). This format includes specific codes for:

  • Account Status: Whether an account is open, closed, paid, etc.
  • Payment History: Whether payments were on time or late
  • Balance: Current amount owed
  • Credit Limit: Maximum credit available

When an account shows "Open" (Status Code 11) on one bureau and "Paid" (Status Code 13) on another, that's a clear Metro 2¬Æ violation. These companies are certified in using this format ‚Äî they know the rules and are expected to follow them.

Section 7: Damages Assessment

This section explains how damages are calculated in FCRA cases and provides our assessment of your potential recovery.

How FCRA Damages Work

The FCRA provides for several types of damages when your rights are violated:

1. Statutory Damages (¬ß 1681n ‚Äî Willful Violations)

For each willful violation, you're entitled to recover between $100 and $1,000 ‚Äî regardless of whether you can prove actual harm. The exact amount within this range is determined by the court based on factors like:

  • The severity of the violation
  • The defendant's conduct
  • Whether the violation was corrected
  • The defendant's financial resources

2. Punitive Damages (¬ß 1681n ‚Äî Willful Violations)

In addition to statutory damages, you may recover punitive damages to punish particularly bad behavior and deter future violations. Courts typically award punitive damages as a multiple of statutory damages (1:1 to 3:1 ratio).

3. Actual Damages (¬ß 1681n and ¬ß 1681o)

If you can prove specific financial harm caused by the violations, you can recover those actual losses. Examples include:

  • Higher interest rates paid on loans
  • Credit application fees for denied applications
  • Lost deposits or opportunities
  • Emotional distress
  • Time spent correcting errors

4. Attorney Fees (¬ß 1681n(a)(3) and ¬ß 1681o(a)(2))

Critically, the defendant must pay your attorney fees if you prevail. This means pursuing valid FCRA claims typically costs you nothing out of pocket.

Appendix A: Glossary of Terms

Appendix A: Glossary of Terms

Actual Damages

Real, documented financial losses caused by FCRA violations, such as higher interest rates, denied applications, or emotional distress.

CRA (Credit Reporting Agency)

Companies that collect and sell consumer credit information. The three major CRAs are Equifax, Experian, and TransUnion.

Data Furnisher

Companies that report your payment history and account information to credit bureaus, such as banks, credit card companies, and lenders.

Dissemination

The sharing of credit information with third parties. Proving dissemination is necessary to establish "standing" in FCRA cases.

FCRA (Fair Credit Reporting Act)

The federal law (15 U.S.C. ¬ß 1681 et seq.) that regulates how consumer credit information is collected, maintained, and shared.

Metro 2¬Æ

The standardized format used by data furnishers to report credit information to credit bureaus. Maintained by the Consumer Data Industry Association (CDIA).

MOV (Method of Verification)

Documentation showing how a credit bureau verified disputed information. Consumers have the right to request this under ¬ß 1681i(a)(7).

Punitive Damages

Additional damages awarded to punish defendants for particularly egregious conduct and deter future violations.

Standing

The legal requirement that a plaintiff must demonstrate actual harm to bring a lawsuit. Established by TransUnion v. Ramirez (2021).

Statutory Damages

Damages set by law ($100-$1,000 per willful FCRA violation) that don't require proof of actual harm.

Willful Violation

A violation committed knowingly or with reckless disregard for the law. Willful violations allow for statutory and punitive damages under ¬ß 1681n.
