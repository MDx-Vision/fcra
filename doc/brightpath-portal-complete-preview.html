üìú The Fair Credit Reporting Act (FCRA)

What Is It?

The FCRA is a federal law passed in 1970 that regulates how your credit information is collected, shared, and used. It gives you specific rights and requires credit bureaus and data furnishers to follow strict accuracy standards.

When these rules are violated, you have the right to take legal action and recover damages ‚Äî that's exactly what we help you do.

Your Core Rights

  • Accuracy ‚Äî Bureaus must maintain "maximum possible accuracy"
  • Dispute ‚Äî You can challenge any inaccurate information
  • Investigation ‚Äî Bureaus must investigate within 30 days
  • Notification ‚Äî You must be told when negative info is reported
  • Damages ‚Äî You can sue and recover money for violations
üîç Types of Violations We Find

These are the most common FCRA violations we identify in credit reports. Each one can result in damages.

üö´ Impossible Contradictions

The same account shows contradictory information across bureaus. Example: "Open" on TransUnion but "Closed" on Experian.

Value: $5,000 ‚Äì $15,000 per violation

üí∞ Balance Discrepancies

Different balances reported across bureaus, or a charged-off account showing an increasing balance (which is illegal).

Value: $3,000 ‚Äì $8,000 per violation

üìÖ Date Errors

Incorrect dates of first delinquency, last payment, or account opening. These can illegally extend how long negative info stays on your report.

Value: $2,000 ‚Äì $6,000 per violation

üîÑ Re-aging Accounts

When a creditor or collector changes dates to make old debt appear newer, keeping it on your report longer than the legal 7-year limit.

Value: $4,000 ‚Äì $12,000 per violation

üë• Mixed Files

Someone else's accounts appearing on your credit report due to similar names, SSNs, or addresses. A serious accuracy failure.

Value: $8,000 ‚Äì $25,000+ per violation

‚è±Ô∏è Failure to Investigate

When a bureau doesn't respond to your dispute within 30 days, or conducts only a superficial "verification" without real investigation.

Value: $3,000 ‚Äì $10,000 per violation

üìñ Glossary of Terms

Common terms you'll encounter during your case.

CRA (Credit Reporting Agency)

The three major bureaus: Equifax, Experian, and TransUnion.

Furnisher

Any company that reports information to credit bureaus (banks, lenders, collectors).

Metro 2 Format

The standardized format creditors must use when reporting to bureaus.

Statutory Damages

Fixed damages ($100-$1,000 per violation) available under FCRA regardless of actual harm.

Willful Violation

When a company knowingly or recklessly violates the law ‚Äî triggers punitive damages.

CFPB

Consumer Financial Protection Bureau ‚Äî the federal agency that oversees credit bureaus.

DOFD (Date of First Delinquency)

The date that starts the 7-year clock for how long negative info can be reported.

Reinsertion

When a deleted item reappears on your report ‚Äî a serious FCRA violation.

‚ùì Frequently Asked Questions
How long does the dispute process take?

Credit bureaus have 30 days to investigate and respond to disputes. The complete process typically takes 60-90 days for most cases, though complex cases with multiple violations may take 3-6 months. We'll keep you updated at every stage through your portal.

What happens if the bureau doesn't respond in 30 days?

If a bureau fails to respond within 30 days, they must remove the disputed item from your credit report. This is also a violation of FCRA ¬ß 1681i that we can use to strengthen your case for damages.

What are "statutory damages" vs "actual damages"?

Statutory damages are fixed amounts ($100-$1,000 per violation) you can recover regardless of proving specific harm. Actual damages require proof of real financial harm (denied credit, lost job, emotional distress). Our approach documents both to maximize your recovery.

Can I dispute items myself for free?

Yes, you have the right to dispute items directly with credit bureaus for free. However, generic disputes often receive automated responses. Our expertise in FCRA law, strategic bundling of violations, and regulatory escalation paths typically achieve better and faster results ‚Äî with potential monetary recovery.

What if the bureau says my dispute is "frivolous"?

Bureaus sometimes label disputes as "frivolous" to avoid investigation. However, they can only do this if your dispute doesn't identify the specific information being disputed. Our letters are detailed and specific, making frivolous claims much harder ‚Äî and when they do, it often becomes evidence of willful non-compliance.

How is this different from "credit repair"?

Traditional credit repair sends generic dispute letters hoping items get deleted. We're building federal litigation cases with documented violations, calculated damages, and regulatory paper trails. This positions you for settlements in the $12,000-$85,000 range, not just item deletions.

Will this hurt my credit score?

No. Disputing inaccurate information does not hurt your credit score. In fact, when inaccurate negative items are removed or corrected, your score typically improves. The dispute process itself has no negative impact on your credit.

What should I do if I receive mail from a credit bureau?

Upload it to your portal immediately! Every response is important documentation. Go to the "Documents" tab and upload the letter. We'll review it and determine next steps. Don't throw anything away.
